## Regional climate envelope (Telangana — Suryapet / Jangaon / Nalgonda triangle)

### Common features (all three districts)
- **Climate type**: Semi-arid, tropical
- **Summer (Mar-Jun)**: 37-41°C peak; minimum 22-26°C
- **Winter (Nov-Feb)**: maximum 24-28°C; minimum 13-18°C
- **Monsoon (Jun-Sep)**: SW monsoon dominant; ~70% of annual rainfall
- **Frost risk**: None (no frost-sensitive constraint)

### Per-district differences

| Dimension | Suryapet | Jangaon | Nalgonda |
|---|---|---|---|
| Annual rainfall | ~731 mm | ~680 mm (drier) | ~750-800 mm (wetter, northern higher) |
| Summer peak | 38-40°C | 39-41°C (hottest of three) | 37-40°C |
| Dominant soil | Red soil + chalka sub-type, some black cotton patches | Black cotton (Regur) dominant; red on outskirts | Mixed: alluvial near Krishna river / red + chalka central / black cotton scattered |
| Major markets | Suryapet local · Hyderabad Monda Market (120 km) · Warangal (90 km) | Jangaon local · **Warangal Wholesale (60 km — primary)** · Hyderabad (150 km) | Nalgonda local · Miryalaguda mandi · Hyderabad Monda (100 km) |
| KVK | Suryapet KVK | Warangal KVK (serves Jangaon) | Nalgonda KVK |
| Historical staple crops | Cotton, paddy, chillies, pulses, mango | Paddy, cotton, chillies, maize | Paddy, cotton, Bengal gram, redgram, chillies, sweet lime (Mosambi) |
| Water sources | Bore wells dominant; some Krishna lift irrigation | Bore wells; limited Manair river access | Krishna river (alluvial belt) · Nagarjuna Sagar canal command area · bore wells |
| Wildlife pressure | Forest-adjacent villages: monkey + wild boar; Nallamala forest edge | Lower forest proximity; less wildlife pressure overall; Pakhal sanctuary edge | Variable — Nallamala forest fringe (south) high pressure; canal belt low |
| Specialty crops | Mango (Banganapalli) — regional reputation; cotton | Chilli (Guntur-area influence); maize | Sweet lime (Mosambi) — Tirumalagiri famous; Bengal gram |

### Implications for crop fit

- **Hass avocado FAILS in all three** (summer crosses 35°C — recommend Fuerte, Pollock, Ettinger, Pinkerton — green-skin varieties)
- **Paddy viable** in canal command (Nalgonda) and bore-well-rich pockets; risky rainfed
- **Drought-tolerant crops** (ragi, jowar, bajra, pulses, sesame, horsegram) viable rainfed in all three
- **Perennials** require drip irrigation for commercial success; subsidized 80-100% under MIDH
- **Black cotton patches** (especially Jangaon) need raised beds for perennials to prevent waterlogging root rot
- **Cotton viable** in all three; Jangaon's black soil is the strongest match

## Soil typology (Telangana detail)

### Red soil (most common in Suryapet, parts of Jangaon, central Nalgonda)
- **Chalka** sub-type: sandy red soil, lighter, well-drained, lower fertility
- **Red loam** sub-type: more clay content, better water-holding
- **pH**: 6.0-7.5 typical
- **Best for**: groundnut, millets, pulses, lemon, mango, drumstick, dragon fruit
- **Constraint**: Lower water retention than black; needs frequent irrigation for water-loving crops

### Black cotton / Regur (dominant in Jangaon, patches in Suryapet + Nalgonda)
- **pH**: 7.5-8.5 alkaline tendency
- **Best for**: cotton, sorghum, soybean, pigeonpea, sunflower, Bengal gram, wheat (irrigated)
- **Constraint**: Heavy clay; waterlogging risk in monsoon — PERENNIALS NEED RAISED BEDS
- **Cracks** in dry season aid root penetration but harm shallow crops

### Alluvial (Krishna river belt of Nalgonda)
- Newer (khadar) near banks; older (bhangar) further out
- High fertility, good for paddy + sugarcane + vegetables + banana
- pH 6.5-7.5
- Best soil among the three districts; underutilized in Telangana relative to UP/Bihar/Bengal

### Saline / alkaline patches (rare but present in older canal command areas)
- Salt-tolerant crops: barley, mustard, dhaincha (green manure), date palm, mesquite

## Variety-level crop economics for Telangana

### LEMON (perennial, 3-year breakeven, 8-year peak)

| Variety | Premium vs Kagzi | Peak ₹/kg | Year 8+ net ₹/acre | Suryapet/Jangaon/Nalgonda fit |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Kagzi Lime (standard) | baseline | ₹30-45 | ₹40-60K | All three good |
| **Thailand Lemon** | **+40-50%** | ₹45-65 | **₹50K-1L** ★ | All three — recommended primary |
| Vikram Seedless | +60-80% | ₹55-75 | ₹70K-1.3L | All three — premium retail/export |
| Pramalini (PKM-1) | +25-35% | ₹40-55 | ₹45-70K | Disease-resistant backup |
| Balaji | +15-20% | ₹35-50 | ₹40-65K | Wholesale + processing focus |

**Telangana structural advantage**: state deficit ~30-40K tons/year → ₹5-15/kg structural premium vs surplus AP.

**Seasonal price windows** (farm gate):
- Peak: April-May (summer heat → demand spike), November (Diwali)
- Trough: July-August (monsoon glut)
- Stable: rest of year

**Suppliers**: SKLTSHU Rajendranagar (govt); Deccan Exotics (Hyderabad, Plot 223 Vivekananda Colony Kukatpally); ICAR-CISH (Lucknow) for Pramalini cuttings.

### AVOCADO (perennial, 3-4 year breakeven, Year 5+ peak)

**Hass DOES NOT WORK** in Telangana (>35°C summer). Recommend:

| Variety | Type | Heat tolerance | Suryapet fit |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fuerte** | Green-skin | High | ★ Primary recommendation |
| Pollock | Green-skin | High (Indian heritage variety since 1941) | ★ |
| Ettinger | Green-skin | High (commercial Israeli) | ★ |
| Pinkerton | Green-skin | High | ★ |
| Hass | Black-skin | Low | ❌ AVOID |

**Rootstock**: Ashdot 17 (Israeli, Phytophthora-resistant) — non-negotiable for commercial planting. Avoid cheap random-seedling grafts.

**Spacing**:
- Standard 6×6m (110-115 trees/acre)
- **Intensive 3.5×7m (165-170 trees/acre) — recommended**
- High-density 500-800 trees/ha

**Economics (1 acre, mature)**:
- Year 1 investment: ₹1.5-2.3 lakh (drip + plants + prep)
- Mature yield: ~7,500 kg/acre (250g avg fruit × 30,000 fruits)
- Wholesale ₹150/kg current → ₹11.25 lakh gross
- Net Year 5+: ₹10-13 lakh/acre

**Critical risks**:
1. **Phytophthora root rot** — kills established trees, no cure. Prevention via raised beds (esp. black soil), well-drained sites, controlled drip, resistant rootstock.
2. **Hot dry winds** — windbreaks (Subabul, Casuarina) on western/southern edges.
3. **Anthracnose** post-harvest in humid pockets — copper sprays from fruit set to harvest.

**Suppliers**: Indo Israel Avocado (Ashdot 17 rootstock); Deccan Exotics (40-acre nursery in Sangareddy); SKLTSHU.

**Recommended pilot**: 0.5-1 acre before scaling, per published Telangana Hort Dept guidance.

### DRAGON FRUIT (perennial, 18-24 month breakeven — fast)

**Suryapet's emerging high-margin crop. Very low water + zero wildlife pressure (cactus).**

| Variety | Type | Color | Premium |
|---|---|---|---|
| Red-flesh Vietnamese | Hylocereus polyrhizus | Magenta | High |
| Red-flesh Israeli | improved varieties | Magenta | Highest |
| White-flesh | Hylocereus undatus | White | Standard |
| Yellow-skin | Selenicereus megalanthus | Yellow | Niche premium |

**Economics**:
- Spacing: 3m × 3m (~450 plants/acre); concrete pole + cactus growing on it
- Year 1 investment: ₹3-5 lakh/acre (poles + plants + drip)
- First fruit: 18-24 months
- Peak production: Year 4+; 8-10 tons/acre
- Wholesale: ₹150-300/kg (Hyderabad retail ₹200-400/kg)
- **Net at peak: ₹3-5 lakh/acre**

**Why Suryapet fit**: tolerates 38-42°C; minimal water (~250mm rainfall-equivalent); negligible pest pressure; **monkeys + wild boar avoid cactus**; **MIDH 50% subsidy on planting material**.

**Suppliers**: Krishi Nursery (Hyderabad); SKLTSHU; Indo Israel Agriculture.

### POMEGRANATE (perennial, 2-3 year breakeven)

| Variety | Trait |
|---|---|
| **Bhagwa** | Export-grade; Maharashtra/AP/Karnataka strong; high premium |
| Ganesh | Older variety, lower yield |

### COTTON (annual, 6-month cycle)

## Exotic / high-value crops (the premium-niche play)

### Aloe Vera
- **Extremely drought-tolerant**, minimal input
- First harvest 12-14 months, multi-year crop
- Sale to cosmetic + medicinal industry; processing units in Hyderabad, Bangalore
- **Net ₹50K-1L/acre/year** at maturity
- Wildlife pressure: zero (bitter)
- **Best for marginal/degraded land** where other crops fail

### Stevia
- Sugar substitute; growing health market
- 6 months to first harvest; multiple harvests/year
- ₹1-2 lakh/acre net at peak
- Requires partial shade in peak summer; intercrop with taller crops

### Lemongrass
- Essential oil extraction; aromatic
- 5+ year perennial; first harvest at 6 months
- ₹60K-1.2 lakh/acre net
- **Native distillation units** in AP/Telangana; Suryapet/Nalgonda viable

### Passion Fruit
- Yellow + Purple varieties
- Trellis system needed; 18-month to bearing
- ₹1.5-2.5 lakh/acre net; premium juice/pulp market
- Wildlife pressure: low

### Fig (Anjeer)
- Poona / Diana / Brown Turkey varieties
- 2 years to bearing; semi-arid native
- ₹1.5-3 lakh/acre net at maturity
- Fresh + dried markets (dried fetches 3x)
- Bird pressure: moderate (netting helps)

### Dates (Khajur)
- Barhi (early-bearing semi-dry) / Medjool (premium soft)
- **Extremely drought-tolerant** (250-400mm)
- 5-7 years to bearing; multi-decade asset
- ₹1.5-3 lakh/acre net at maturity; Gujarat/Rajasthan model
- Telangana experimental — early adopters can corner regional supply

### Moringa (commercial drumstick)
- PKM-1 / Bhagya / Coimbatore-2 varieties
- 6 months to first harvest
- Triple revenue: fresh pods (₹40K/acre), leaves powder export (₹50K-1L/acre), seeds (₹20K/acre)
- **Zero wildlife pressure**; very low water; rainfed-feasible
- Boundary crop OR primary crop at scale

### Olive
- Arbequina / Koroneiki varieties
- 3-5 years to bearing; experimental in Telangana
- Rajasthan + MP pilots; cooking oil market
- ₹1-2 lakh/acre net (variable, experimental status)

**Per cow economics**:
- Daily milk × ₹40-60/L = ₹400-1200 daily revenue
- Feed + vet + labor: ₹250-500/day
- **Net ₹150-700/cow/day = ₹4.5-21K/cow/month**
- Manure: 20-25 kg/day → free fertilizer for ~1 acre
- Calving: ~1 calf/year (additional asset)

**Govt schemes**: NLM (National Livestock Mission, 25-50% on indigenous breeds), RKVY, NDDB linkages for milk procurement (Vijaya, Heritage, Mother Dairy).

### APIARY (BEE KEEPING)

| Species | Behavior | Yield/box | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Apis cerana indica** (native) | Smaller hive, hardy, defensive | 8-15 kg/year | Recommended for beginners; native pollinators |
| **Apis mellifera** (Italian) | Larger hive, gentler, higher yield | 20-30 kg/year | Requires more management; commercial scale |
| Trigona (stingless) | Very small, niche | 1-3 kg/year | Medicinal honey premium |

**Economics**:
- Box cost: ₹3-5K each (with bee colony)
- Annual maintenance: ₹500-1000/box
- Honey wholesale: ₹150-300/kg
- **Per box revenue (mellifera): 25 kg × ₹250 = ₹6,250/year**
- **MIDH subsidy: ₹2,000/box (50%) up to 20 boxes**

**Pollination benefit to neighboring crops** (often overlooked):
- Mango, lemon, avocado, drumstick: 20-40% yield boost
- Tomato, mustard, sunflower, sesame: 25-50% yield boost
- Pomegranate, dragon fruit: significant benefit

**Placement**: Within 100-200m of flowering crops; relocate seasonally.

**Suppliers + training**: KVK apiculture programs; Mohan Bee Keeping (Pune); KVIC nodal officers.

### POULTRY

- **Backyard / desi (Aseel, Kadaknath, Vanaraja)**: 50-150 birds, low input, ₹15-30K revenue/year, manure for crops
- **Commercial layer**: 500+ hens, ₹3-5L investment, ₹50K-1L net/year, high disease risk
- **Commercial broiler**: 4-5 cycles/year, ₹1-2L net/cycle at scale
- **Telangana Aseel** known for fighting cock genetics + premium meat market

### FISH (POND AQUACULTURE)

- **Indian Major Carps (rohu, catla, mrigal)**: ₹40-50K pond setup, ₹80K-1.5L revenue/year
- **Pangasius (intensive)**: higher yield, ₹2-3L net possible but more management
- **Tilapia**: profitable in Telangana climate
- Nalgonda canal belt has tank infrastructure — fish farming subsidy via Fisheries Dept

### SERICULTURE (silk farming)

- Mulberry-based; AP has strongest support; Telangana developing
- ₹40-60K/acre net at maturity; cocoon prices govt-regulated floor
- Labor-intensive (cocoon harvest every 25-30 days)
- Department of Sericulture training + nursery support

### MUSHROOM

- **Oyster mushroom** (Pleurotus ostreatus): low investment ₹20-30K, 30-day cycle, urban demand
- **Milky mushroom** (Calocybe indica): tropical-friendly, higher temp tolerance
- **Button mushroom**: needs controlled-environment, higher investment ₹2-5L

## SUSTAINABILITY PRACTICES

### Zero-Budget Natural Farming (ZBNF / Natural Farming)

Subhash Palekar's framework. Strong govt support in AP (Rythu Sadhikara Samstha — RySS); Telangana growing programs.

**4 wheels**:
1. **Jeevamruta** — microbial culture (cow dung + urine + jaggery + pulse flour + soil)
2. **Bijamruta** — seed treatment with same microbial mix
3. **Achhadana (mulching)** — straw, leaves, intercrop residues
4. **Whaphasa (moisture)** — soil moisture conservation via mulching + minimal tillage

**Economics**: 70-80% input cost reduction; yields stabilize at 80-90% of conventional after 2-3 transition years.

**Certification path**: PGS-India (Participatory Guarantee System) for community-scale; NPOP for export-grade. Organic certification takes 3 years.

### Agroforestry

- **Subabul + cotton**: Subabul (Leucaena) fixes N, provides fuelwood + fodder
- **Drumstick + ragi**: Boundary perennial + drought-tolerant cereal
- **Mango + pulses**: 5-year window before mango canopy closes
- **Coconut + banana + black pepper**: 3-tier in Kerala; less common Telangana
- **Casuarina + crops**: Windbreak + 5-year pulp wood harvest

**Carbon credit potential**: Verra (VCS), NABARD pilots, India Carbon Market (in development).

### Rainwater harvesting

- **Farm pond** (3-5m × 3-5m × 2m): NREGA-eligible (free labor for sanctioned schemes)
- **Check dams**: community-scale; Telangana Mission Kakatiya program
- **Rooftop harvesting**: limited but possible

**Storage**: ~1 acre-foot per 1000 sqm catchment per heavy rain event.

### Solar pumps

- **PM-KUSUM scheme**: 60-90% subsidy; individual farmer + community installations
- Replaces diesel pumps; OPEX → zero after install
- Surplus solar can sell to grid in some states (not yet Telangana)

### Biogas

- 2-4 cattle = enough manure for 1 cum biogas plant
- Plant cost: ₹35-40K; MNRE subsidy 50-90%
- Output: cooking gas (replaces 2-3 LPG cylinders/month) + slurry (high-quality compost)

### Composting + vermicomposting

- Vermicompost (Eisenia foetida earthworms): ₹500-1000/quintal sale price; on-farm production
- Cattle manure compost: free fertilizer; key ZBNF input

### Drip irrigation

- **MIDH subsidy: 80-100%** depending on category (SC/ST/marginal/small farmer get 100%)
- 30-50% water savings vs flood
- Critical for perennials in Telangana

### Mulching

- Straw, paddy husk, dry leaves
- 50-70% reduction in evaporation
- Free input from on-farm residues

### Mixed / intercrop / rotation patterns (Telangana-specific)

| Pattern | Region | Logic |
|---|---|---|
| Pigeonpea + sorghum | Deccan | Tall + short, different roots, pigeonpea N-fixing |
| Maize + cowpea | All three districts | Pulse fixes N for cereal |
| Cotton + greengram | Jangaon | Greengram harvested before cotton peaks |
| Sugarcane + onion | Canal command | Onion in inter-rows, short cycle |

| Pearl millet + cluster bean | Dry pockets | Both drought-tolerant |

### 1. Paddy (Yield Optimizer deep-dive)

Paddy (Oryza sativa) is Telangana's largest single crop by acreage. The Yield Optimizer for paddy must reconcile three competing objectives: (1) maximize per-acre yield, (2) minimize water consumption (irrigation tariffs + KRMB constraints), (3) maintain grain quality for market premium (especially Sona Masuri grade).

Conventional Telangana paddy management still leaves 30-50% yield on the table relative to best-practice SRI / well-managed transplanted systems. The biggest single lever is **nitrogen split discipline** followed by **water regime selection** followed by **timely IPM** at panicle initiation.

#### 1.1 Water Regime Selection

The choice of water regime drives water consumption, labor, weed pressure, and yield ceiling. Pick one and commit — half-measures (e.g., DSR plus continuous flood) destroy the economics.

**SRI (System of Rice Intensification)**
- Young seedlings: transplant at 8-12 days old (vs conventional 25-30 days). 2-leaf stage is the marker.
- Wide spacing: 25 × 25 cm single seedling per hill (vs conventional 15 × 10 cm, 2-3 seedlings).
- Alternate Wetting-Drying: keep field moist, not flooded, until panicle initiation; then shallow flood until grain fill.
- Mechanical weeding: cono-weeder or rotary weeder every 10-12 days for first 35 DAT (incorporates weeds as green manure).
- Target: 1.5-2.0 t/acre **more** than conventional, with 30-40% less water.
- Caveat: requires labor discipline at transplanting; not viable in fully-mechanized systems unless using SRI transplanters.

**DSR (Direct Seeded Rice)**
- Drill (preferred) or broadcast pre-germinated seed onto puddled or unpuddled field.
- Seed rate: 8-10 kg/acre drilled, 12-15 kg/acre broadcast.
- 25-30% lower water use vs continuous flood.
- Suitable for canal-command areas with reliable but limited water (e.g., SRSP ayacut tail-end).
- Critical: **weed management is make-or-break** — pre-emergent pendimethalin within 3 days of sowing, plus post-emergent bispyribac-sodium at 18-20 DAS.
- Yield: 3.5-4.5 t/acre (~10-15% below transplanted under good management, but lower input cost).

**AWD (Alternate Wetting Drying)**
- Continuous flood up to active tillering, then let field drain to 15 cm below soil (use a perforated pipe / field water tube), re-irrigate when water drops to that depth.
- Re-flood at panicle initiation and maintain shallow flood through grain fill.
- Water saving: 15-20% with no yield penalty (often slight positive).
- Methane emission reduction: 30-50% — relevant for any carbon-credit aggregator (e.g., Boomitra, Nurture.farm).

**Continuous Flood (conventional)**
- 5-10 cm standing water from transplanting through grain fill, drained 7-10 days pre-harvest.
- Highest water use (~15-18 ML/acre); highest methane footprint.
- Default for canal command in upper Krishna / Godavari ayacut. Easy to manage; weed pressure low.
- Yield 3-4 t/acre conventional; ceiling ~4.5 t/acre even with strong fertility.

#### 1.3 Nitrogen Split Schedule

Total recommended N: 120-150 kg urea-equivalent per acre on medium-fertility black or red soils. (For a 130 kg N target, that's ~280 kg urea or split across multiple sources.)

**Split schedule (the single highest-leverage operation):**

| Stage | DAT (Days After Transplanting) | % of total N | Source | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Basal | 0 (at transplanting) | 25% | DAP (50 kg) + MOP (35 kg) + 25% urea | Incorporate before puddling final pass |
| Tillering | 21-25 DAT | 35% | Urea broadcast on moist (not flooded) soil | Drain 24h before, re-flood 24h after |
| Panicle initiation | 50-60 DAT | 25% | Urea + foliar 0.5% ZnSO4 | Critical for spikelet number |
| Booting | 70-80 DAT | 15% | Urea (skip if leaf color chart shows >4) | Drives grain weight |

**Use a Leaf Color Chart (LCC):** apply N top-dress only if leaf score ≤ 4. Saves 15-20 kg urea/acre on average, reduces BPH attraction.

**Foliar supplements:**
- ZnSO4 0.5% spray at panicle initiation (corrects almost-universal Zn deficiency in Telangana soils).
- KH2PO4 1% at grain fill if visible yellowing — corrects late-season K deficiency, common in continuous-paddy plots.
- Silicon (potassium silicate or rice husk ash) — strengthens stems, reduces lodging and stem borer entry.

**Why splits matter:** A single basal application of all the N wastes 40-60% to volatilization + leaching, attracts BPH (which thrives on high foliar N), and lodges the crop. The 4-split schedule keeps soil N concentration moderate throughout, matches crop demand curve, and protects against pest amplification.

#### 1.4 Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

**Brown Planthopper (BPH, Nilaparvata lugens)** — the #1 yield destroyer in Telangana paddy
- Scout twice weekly from active tillering. Tap the base of 10 hills per acre; count nymphs+adults.
- Action threshold: ≥10/hill (or 5/hill if natural enemies absent).
- Do NOT use synthetic pyrethroids (lambda-cyhalothrin, cypermethrin) — they kill BPH predators (mirid bugs, spiders) and cause resurgence.
- Recommended chemistry: imidacloprid 17.8% SL at 60 ml/acre, OR pymetrozine 50% WG at 120 g/acre, OR dinotefuran 20% SG at 80 g/acre.
- Rotate chemistries between sprays (BPH develops resistance fast).
- Cultural: avoid over-fertilization with N; maintain alternate wet-dry rather than continuous flood; alley-cutting (3 ft gap every 12 ft) improves air circulation.

**Yellow stem borer (Scirpophaga incertulas)**
- Pheromone traps: 5 traps/acre, placed at panicle height. Action when catch >5/trap/week.
- Trichogramma japonicum (egg parasitoid) release at 50,000/acre/release, 3 releases at weekly intervals starting tillering.
- Chemical: chlorantraniliprole 0.4% GR at 4 kg/acre OR cartap hydrochloride 4% GR at 10 kg/acre (apply into shallow standing water).

**Blast (Pyricularia oryzae)**
- Scout for diamond-shaped lesions with gray center on leaves; neck blast is yield-killer.
- Preventive at boot stage: tricyclazole 75% WP at 120 g/acre + foliar fertilizer.
- Resistance: BPT 5204 is susceptible; rotate fungicide groups (tricyclazole → kasugamycin) if multiple sprays needed.
- Avoid late evening irrigation that creates leaf wetness overnight.

**Sheath blight (Rhizoctonia solani)**
- Lesions on lower leaf sheaths, then upward. Worst in dense canopies + high N.
- Validamycin 3% L at 400 ml/acre at active tillering and again 15 days later.
- Cultural: alley-cutting, balanced N, avoid >50 hills/m².

**Bacterial leaf blight (Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae)**
- Linear yellow-to-brown lesions starting from leaf tip.
- No effective chemical; use copper oxychloride 50% WP at 600 g/acre as bacteriostatic.
- Resistant varieties: WGL 32100, IR 64. Avoid mechanical injury (high-clearance equipment).

**False smut (Ustilaginoidea virens)**
- Greenish-yellow spore balls replace grains. Worsening problem in Telangana due to high humidity + N excess.
- Preventive: propiconazole 25% EC at 200 ml/acre at booting and 50% flowering.

#### 1.5 Drainage Discipline (Pre-Harvest)

- Drain field 7-10 days before harvest for combine access and to harden grain.
- Earlier drainage = lower grain weight; later drainage = sunken combines + 5-8% mechanical loss.
- Target moisture at harvest: 20-22% (drying afterward to 14% for storage).
- Combine throughput at 22% moisture is 30-40% faster than at 26%; grain breakage <2% vs 6-8%.

#### 1.6 Yield Benchmarks

| Management level | Yield (t/acre) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Conventional flood, no splits, no IPM | 3.0-3.5 | Default Telangana baseline |
| Conventional flood, 4-split N, basic IPM | 4.0-4.5 | "Good farmer" — common with KVK contact |
| AWD + 4-split N + IPM + LCC | 4.5-5.0 | Modest water saving, no yield loss |
| SRI (full protocol) | 5.0-7.0 | Best-in-class; labor-intensive |
| Best farm + SRI + premium variety | 7.0-8.0 | Achievable with all levers pulled |

**Economic uplift of moving from 3.5 to 5.0 t/acre:** +1.5 t × ₹2,200/q × 10 = ₹33,000/acre additional gross. Incremental cost of management (extra fertilizer splits, scouting, IPM) ~₹4,000-6,000/acre. Net uplift ₹27,000-29,000/acre — among the highest ROI improvements available in Telangana agriculture.

### 2. Mango (Yield Optimizer deep-dive)

Mango (Mangifera indica) in Telangana is dominated by the Suryapet-Nalgonda-Khammam belt, with Banganapalli and Himayat (Imam Pasand) being the commercially significant varieties. The Yield Optimizer must manage the **alternate-bearing problem** (heavy crop one year, near-zero the next), drive **off-season flowering** for premium pricing, and protect against the **fruit fly + mealybug** complex that can destroy 30-60% of crop at the panicle/fruit stage.

The single highest-leverage intervention in mango is **paclobutrazol (PBZ) protocol** — used correctly, it doubles realizable yield over a 5-year window by smoothing alternate bearing. Used incorrectly (overdosed, repeated yearly), it kills tree vigor and shortens orchard life.

#### 2.1 Variety Catalog (Telangana Focus)

| Variety | Maturity | Yield (t/acre, bearing) | Price tier (₹/kg) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Banganapalli (Safeda)** | Mid-season (May-Jun) | 5-7 | 40-80 | Suryapet specialty; large fruit, sweet, good shelf life |
| **Himayat (Imam Pasand)** | Late (Jun-Jul) | 3-5 | 120-300 | GI-tagged Telangana; premium local; smaller fruit, intense flavor |
| **Dashehari** | Early-mid (May-Jun) | 4-6 | 50-90 | North India favorite; adapts well to Telangana |
| **Alphonso (Hapus)** | Mid (May-Jun) | 3-5 | 200-500 | Export quality; needs specific microclimate (Konkan-like); not universally suited |
| **Kesar** | Mid-late (Jun) | 4-6 | 80-150 | Saffron color; Gujarat origin; Telangana adapts; good for pulp industry |
| **Totapuri** | Late (Jul-Aug) | 8-10 | 20-30 | Highest yield; pulp/juice industry only; low table value |
| **Mallika** | Late (Jul-Aug) | 4-6 | 80-120 | Hybrid (Neelum × Dashehari); regular bearer; emerging in Telangana |
| **Amrapali** | Late (Jul-Aug) | 6-8 (high density) | 60-100 | Dwarf; suited to HDP at 5m × 5m |

**Selection logic:**
- Established Suryapet orchard, mandi-driven → Banganapalli (default).
- High-value direct sales / online / urban premium → Himayat or Alphonso.
- New plantation, want regular bearing + dwarf habit → Amrapali or Mallika.
- Pulp contract (Coca-Cola Maaza, Foods & Inns) → Totapuri.

#### 2.2 Paclobutrazol (PBZ) Protocol

PBZ (brand: Cultar, Paclo, others) is a gibberellin biosynthesis inhibitor. By suppressing vegetative flush in the off-bearing year, it forces the tree to commit carbohydrate to flowering, smoothing alternate-year bearing and synchronizing flush for predictable harvest timing.

**Standard protocol:**
- **Timing:** Soil drench in September (2 months before natural flowering flush of December-January).
- **Dose:** 5 g active ingredient (a.i.) per year of tree age, multiplied by variety factor.
- **Variety factor:** Alphonso 1.2× · Banganapalli 1.0× · Himayat 0.9× · Totapuri 1.1× · Dashehari 0.8×.
- **Application:** Dissolve in 5 L water, drench in 12-15 cm trench at canopy drip-line. Avoid trunk area.
- **Irrigation discipline:** STOP all irrigation for 2 weeks post-application. Then resume light irrigation only after first flowering panicles appear.
- **Foliar booster:** KNO3 1% spray 4 weeks post-PBZ to enhance flower bud differentiation.

**Worked example (10-year-old Banganapalli tree):**
- Dose: 10 years × 5 g × 1.0 = 50 g a.i. per tree.
- If using 23% SC formulation: 50 g a.i. ÷ 0.23 = ~218 ml product per tree.
- 70 trees/acre at 8 × 8 m spacing → 218 × 70 = 15.2 L product/acre.
- Cost at ₹1,200/L = ₹18,200/acre per application. Apply in off-year only.

**Expected impact:**
- Forces uniform flowering across the orchard (synchronizes harvest, reduces fruit-fly window).
- Converts alternate-year bearing to near-regular bearing.
- Suppresses unproductive vegetative flush.

**Risks and what NOT to do:**
- DO NOT apply in a bearing year — wastes hormone and overstresses tree.
- DO NOT apply yearly without skip; cumulative PBZ residue in soil persists 2-3 years.
- DO NOT overdose; symptoms are excessive dwarfing, brittle panicles, fruit drop, reduced leaf size.
- DO NOT apply on trees < 5 years old (still in formative phase).
- Excessive vegetative suppression → orchard "running out of steam" by Year 12-15 of treatment.

#### 2.3 Off-Season Strategy (May-June flowering instead of Dec-Jan)

Off-season mango fetches 5-8× the price of peak-season mango in Hyderabad / Mumbai markets. The strategy combines pruning timing, PBZ, and controlled water-stress to push flowering from natural December-January to May-June, delivering fruit August-October when market is starved.

**Timeline:**
1. **Post-harvest (June-July of bearing year):** Light pruning — remove water sprouts, dead wood, crossing branches. Allow new flush to develop.
2. **August:** Foliar 0.5% KNO3 + micronutrient spray. Encourage flush hardening.
3. **September:** PBZ application as per Section 2.2.
4. **September-November:** Withhold irrigation completely. Allow tree to experience water stress.
5. **December-January:** Light irrigation resumes only after first flower buds visible. Avoid flush stimulation.
6. **January-February:** Flowering completes. Apply 19:19:19 foliar (1%) for fruit set.
7. **March-April:** Fruit development. Heavy irrigation begins. Mulch + KNO3 foliar.
8. **May-June:** Harvest at off-season premium.

**Economics:**
- Additional investment: ₹8,000-12,000/acre (PBZ + extra irrigation labor + extra spray cycles).
- Price uplift: ₹150-250/kg vs ₹40-80/kg peak season for Banganapalli.
- On 5 t/acre off-season at ₹180/kg = ₹9 L/acre gross vs ₹3 L/acre on-season — a 3× revenue uplift.
- Caveat: not every tree responds; success rate 60-75% first attempt, improves with practice.

#### 2.4 Pruning by Tree Age

Mango pruning is age-stage specific. Wrong pruning destroys yield; right pruning rejuvenates productivity.

**Years 1-3 (formative):**
- Central leader removal at 75-100 cm height (forces lateral branching).
- Select 3-4 scaffold branches at 45-60° angles, evenly spaced around trunk.
- Tip back scaffolds to 60 cm to encourage secondary branching.
- Remove all flowers in Years 1-2 (channel energy to structure).

**Years 3-7 (development):**
- Maintain open center (vase shape) — allows light penetration to lower canopy.
- Remove crossing branches, water sprouts, downward growth.
- Light tipping of leaders to maintain canopy at 4-5 m height (manageable harvest).
- Begin allowing fruit (Year 3 = 5-10 fruits/tree, Year 5 = 30-50 fruits/tree).

**Years 7-15 (peak bearing):**
- Annual light maintenance pruning post-harvest.
- Remove 10-15% canopy / year.
- Open center; maintain light penetration with summer pruning.

**Years 15+ (rejuvenation):**
- Hard prune 40-50% of canopy in February (post-fruit-set, before monsoon).
- Whitewash exposed branches with lime+copper to prevent sunburn.
- Skip fruit harvest year of pruning (Year 1).
- Recovers full productivity by Year 3 post-rejuvenation.
- Total orchard productive life can extend to 40-50 years with one or two rejuvenation cycles.

#### 2.5 Pollination Management

Mango is primarily insect-pollinated (also wind-pollinated, but bee-mediated pollination dramatically improves fruit set in still air conditions).

- **2-3 Apis mellifera bee boxes per acre**, placed at 20-25% flowering.
- Position boxes at orchard edge, facing east (catches morning warmth).
- Expected: 25-40% improvement in fruit set, especially in shaded/interior canopy.
- DO NOT spray insecticides during peak flowering (08:00-15:00 hours specifically) — kills bees.
- If using a chemical spray for hopper/mealybug control during flowering, time it to early evening (18:00+) when bee activity is minimal.

For Telangana farmers with apiary integration: 2 mango acres + 6 bee boxes typically produces 30-40 kg honey/season as a byproduct of pollination service (₹400-600/kg = ₹15-25K additional income).

#### 2.6 Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

**Mealybug (Drosicha mangiferae and Rastrococcus invadens)**
- Stem-banding with sticky polythene + grease at 30 cm height during February (prevents crawler migration up trunk).
- Soil drench with chlorpyrifos 20% EC at 5 ml/L around base in February-March.
- Sticky traps at canopy level.
- Severe infestation: foliar buprofezin 25% SC at 2 ml/L.

**Mango hopper (Idioscopus clypealis, Amritodus atkinsoni)**
- Imidacloprid 17.8% SL at 0.3 ml/L OR thiamethoxam 25% WG at 0.2 g/L at panicle emergence.
- Repeat at 50% flowering if hopper count >5/panicle.
- Avoid synthetic pyrethroids — they flare mites and kill bees.

**Fruit fly (Bactrocera dorsalis, Bactrocera correcta)**
- Methyl eugenol traps at 50/ha (1 every 14 m grid).
- Protein hydrolysate bait spray: 10 ml protein hydrolysate + 2 ml malathion 50% EC + 1 L water; spot-spray on undersides of 5-6 leaves per tree weekly.
- Fallen fruit collection + deep burial (breaks the life cycle).
- Bagging of high-value fruits (Himayat, Alphonso) with butter paper bags from marble stage — 100% fly protection.

**Anthracnose (Colletotrichum gloeosporioides)**
- Hexaconazole 5% EC at 1 ml/L at panicle emergence, again at fruit pea stage.
- Copper oxychloride 50% WP at 2 g/L at flowering (also covers bacterial spot).
- Post-harvest hot water dip (52°C for 5 minutes) extends shelf life and reduces latent anthracnose.

**Powdery mildew (Oidium mangiferae)**
- Wettable sulfur 80% WP at 3 g/L at first symptom.
- Avoid during fruit-near-maturity stage (sulfur residue).

#### 2.7 Yield Benchmarks

| Management level | Yield (t/acre) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Neglected (rainfed, no pruning, no IPM) | 2-4 | Common in older orchards |
| Basic management (occasional pruning, basic spray) | 4-6 | "Average farmer" |
| Good management (annual pruning, full IPM, fertilizer) | 6-10 | KVK-supported orchards |
| PBZ + good management | 10-13 | Smooths alternate bearing |
| Off-season + PBZ + intensive | 12-15 | Best-case Telangana commercial |

**Note on alternate bearing:** A tree yielding 15 t/acre in a bearing year and 0 t/acre in the off-year averages 7.5 t/acre. PBZ converts that to 10 + 8 = 9 t/acre average — a 20% lift just from smoothing.

### 3. Corn / Maize (Yield Optimizer deep-dive)

Maize (Zea mays) in Telangana is grown both kharif (June-October) and rabi (November-March). The Yield Optimizer for maize is dominated by two factors: (1) **Fall Armyworm (FAW) management** — a 2018-arrived pest that without aggressive IPM destroys 30-60% of unmanaged crops, and (2) **hybrid selection** appropriate to season + buyer (poultry feed vs starch vs export).

Wildlife pressure (monkeys, wild boar) is also a first-class constraint in forest-adjacent farms — without exclusion, 30-60% of crop is lost regardless of agronomy.

#### 3.1 Fall Armyworm (Spodoptera frugiperda) Protocol

FAW was first reported in India in 2018 (Karnataka), and is now endemic across India including Telangana. Females lay 100-200 eggs per mass on undersides of leaves; larvae feed in whorls causing characteristic "shotgun hole" damage; severe infestation kills growing point.

**Monitoring:**
- Pheromone traps on a 25 m × 25 m grid (16 traps/acre). Use commercial FAW-specific lures (PCI, Bio-Vector, ATGC).
- Visual scouting twice weekly from V2 stage. Inspect whorls of 20 plants per acre.
- **Action threshold:** ≥5% plants with fresh damage OR ≥1 larva per plant.

**Spray windows (critical):**
- V2-V4 (early whorl): most vulnerable; spray decisions here determine whole-season trajectory.
- V5-V6 (late whorl): last good window; once tasseling begins, sprays into whorl no longer effective.
- After tasseling (VT+): sprays are largely cosmetic — FAW damage to leaves is tolerated, but cob damage is hard to control with foliar.

**Organic-first ladder:**
1. **Neem seed kernel extract (NSKE) 5%** — 50 g neem powder per L water, soak 12 h, filter, spray into whorl. Apply weekly from V2.
2. **Bt sprays** (Bacillus thuringiensis var. kurstaki at 1 g/L) — apply evening; effective on small larvae only (1st-2nd instar).
3. **Hand-removal of egg masses** — labor-intensive but very effective on small plots; egg masses are easy to spot (fuzzy gray-white patches under leaves).
4. **Sand + ash mixture** poured into whorl — physical disruption of newly-hatched larvae. Old farmer technique; works.

**Chemical (severe infestation, >10% damage):**
- **Emamectin benzoate 5% SG** at 4 g/15 L → 80 g/acre. Most effective FAW chemistry currently. Apply DIRECTLY INTO WHORL (not onto leaves) — use a hollow-cone nozzle held over each plant.
- Spinosad 45% SC at 0.4 ml/L (organic-certified option, effective).
- Chlorantraniliprole 18.5% SC at 0.4 ml/L (long residual, but expensive).
- Avoid: synthetic pyrethroids (resistance, kill natural enemies).

**Cultural / habitat manipulation:**
- **Intercrop with cowpea or groundnut** at every 4th row — disrupts FAW host-finding (mixed odor cues confuse moth), provides nectar for parasitoids (Cotesia, Telenomus).
- Avoid late sowing (>15 days after recommended window) — late-sown maize is more vulnerable to FAW peak.
- Plant in solid block with neighbors (synchronized sowing reduces continuous food supply for the moth).
- Push-pull system: napier grass border + desmodium intercrop — push FAW with desmodium (volatile repellents), pull to napier border. ICIPE-tested; works in East Africa, being trialed in India.

#### 3.3 Wildlife Deterrents (Monkey + Wild Boar)

In forest-adjacent farms (within 2-3 km of reserve forest or perennial water source), wildlife is the dominant yield constraint, often more important than any agronomic factor.

**Solar-powered electric fence**
- ₹40,000-60,000/acre perimeter cost (depends on shape; rectangular cheaper than irregular).
- 3-wire system: ground wire + 2 hot wires at 60 cm and 120 cm.
- Solar panel (50W) + battery + energizer. Maintenance: clear grass touching wires weekly.
- Life: 7-10 years if maintained.
- Effective vs wild boar (>90%), partial vs monkeys (they jump over; 40-60% deterrent).

**Trip-wire LPG bangers (gas guns)**
- ₹15,000-25,000 unit cost; fires loud crack at random intervals (5-15 min).
- Effective for first 7-14 days, then animals habituate.
- Use 2-3 units rotated through different locations.

**Reflective scarecrows + tape**
- Cheap (₹1,000-2,000/acre); only effective in first week of crop visibility.
- Use as supplement, not standalone.

**Border crop deterrents**
- Bitter gourd, chili (Bhut Jolokia or Guntur Sannam), tobacco — repel monkeys and wild boar via taste.
- Plant 2-3 m wide border. Becomes additional income source.

**Community vigilance**
- Pooled labor: 2-3 farmers share night-watch for adjacent maize plots. Most effective when combined with bangers / lights.

**Strategic intercrop**
- Avoid pure maize block in high-pressure zones.
- Alternate maize with sesame / pigeon pea — wildlife less attracted to mixed plantings.

#### 3.4 Nitrogen Management

Maize is one of the most N-hungry field crops — total demand 100-120 kg N/acre for hybrids in irrigated conditions.

| Stage | DAS (Days After Sowing) | % of total N | Source | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Basal | 0 (at sowing) | 30% | NPK 12:32:16 at 100 kg/acre | Drilled with seed (5 cm away) |
| Knee-high (V6) | 25-30 DAS | 40% | Urea 80 kg/acre top-dress | Band 7-10 cm from plant row |
| Tasseling (VT) | 50-55 DAS | 30% | Urea 60 kg/acre OR foliar 1.5% | Critical for grain fill |

**Sulfur:** 15-20 kg/acre at basal — applied as ammonium sulfate or single super phosphate. Strongly correlated with grain protein and oil content (matters for feed buyers).

**Zinc:** 10 kg ZnSO4/acre at basal OR foliar 0.5% at V6.

**Boron:** 1-2 kg borax/acre at basal (only if soil-test low) — improves grain set on cob tip.

**Soil test driven adjustments:**
- Available P >50 ppm: reduce DAP by half.
- Available K >250 ppm (black cotton): skip MOP entirely.
- pH >8.5: foliar Zn instead of soil application (Zn unavailable at high pH).

#### 3.5 Other Key Pests

**Stem borer (Chilo partellus)**
- Early instar damage: pinholes on whorl leaves, "dead heart" on growing point.
- Granular insecticide (chlorantraniliprole 0.4G) at 4-5 kg/acre applied INTO whorl at V3-V5. NOTE: carbofuran has been prohibited/restricted in India — use only CIB&RC-current approved alternatives.
- Trichogramma chilonis release: 50,000/acre at 10 and 25 DAS.

**Maize aphid (Rhopalosiphum maidis)**
- Common at tasseling; sucks sap, deposits honeydew, sooty mold develops.
- Foliar thiamethoxam 25% WG at 0.2 g/L (one spray usually sufficient).
- Avoid sprays during pollination (pollinator damage).

**Pink stem borer (Sesamia inferens)**
- Late-season; bores into stems, lodging risk.
- Pheromone trap monitoring at flowering; spray if catch >10/trap/week.
- Same chemistry as Chilo.

**Banded leaf and sheath blight (Rhizoctonia solani)**
- High humidity disease; lesions on lower sheaths spread upward.
- Hexaconazole 5% EC at 1 ml/L at first symptom.
- Cultural: avoid excessive plant density (>72,000 plants/acre).

### 4. Cotton (Yield Optimizer deep-dive)

Cotton (Gossypium hirsutum) in Telangana is overwhelmingly Bt cotton — virtually no farmer plants conventional. The Yield Optimizer for cotton must address: (1) **mandatory refuge strategy** for pink bollworm resistance management (regulatory + practical), (2) **HDPS (High Density Planting System)** as the dominant productivity uplift lever, (3) **drip fertigation** for high-yield management, (4) the **IPM ladder** for the bollworm complex + whitefly + thrips.

Telangana cotton economics have been challenging the last 5 years due to pink bollworm Bt resistance breakdown. Refuge compliance + IPM discipline are now genuinely existential for the crop, not optional.

#### 4.1 Refuge Strategy (Mandatory 2026)

The Indian regulatory framework (Insecticide Resistance Management policy + CICR guidelines) now requires refuge compliance with penalties for non-compliance. Seed companies supply non-Bt seed in every Bt seed packet (the "Refuge in Bag" or RIB system), or as a separate marked sachet.

**Mechanism:**
- Plant 5-10% non-Bt cotton as a border row or random mix.
- Susceptible pink bollworm (PBW) from refuge mate with resistant PBW from Bt field.
- Offspring are heterozygous, still susceptible to Bt toxin.
- Slows the evolution of resistance, extending the Bt technology lifetime.

**Implementation:**
- Plant non-Bt seed in 2-row border around field perimeter, OR mix in seed packet (RIB).
- **DO NOT spray insecticide on refuge rows** — defeats the purpose by killing the susceptible PBW.
- Pheromone traps (PBW-specific Gossyplure) at 5/ha to monitor PBW population.

**Why it matters in 2026:**
- Bt resistance breakdown in PBW is documented across Gujarat, Maharashtra, Telangana.
- Without refuge discipline, expected useful life of Bt technology in cotton is 8-12 years (already past in some districts).
- With refuge: 5-10 additional years of resistance delay.

#### 4.2 HDPS (High Density Planting System)

HDPS is the single highest-leverage productivity lever in Telangana cotton, delivering 15-30% yield increase over conventional spacing.

**Spacing comparison:**

| System | Row × plant spacing | Plants/acre | Yield expectation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conventional | 90 × 60 cm | 7,000-10,000 | 8-12 q/acre |
| HDPS | 60 × 10-15 cm | 18,000-25,000 | 18-24 q/acre |
| Ultra-HDPS | 45 × 10 cm | 32,000+ | 22-28 q/acre (drip mandatory) |

**Requirements:**
- **Drip + fertigation** — essential. Conventional flood is not feasible at HDPS density (water logging, fungal pressure).
- **Compact / early varieties** — Bunny Bt, RCH 2, JK 1947, Solar 65. Tall/rangy varieties unsuitable.
- **Manual spray boom or knapsack** — wide-row tractor sprayers don't fit; foot-operated power sprayers preferred.
- **Defoliation** at harvest with ethephon (HDPS is too dense for hand-picking efficiency without leaf removal).

**Not suitable for:**
- Pure rainfed without irrigation backup (HDPS crop crashes if 15+ day dry spell hits).
- Fully manual labor systems (manual harvest of 24,000 plants/acre is labor-prohibitive without defoliation).

**Investment:**
- Drip installation: ₹35,000-50,000/acre (one-time; lasts 7-10 years with maintenance).
- Seed cost: ~₹2,500/acre extra (higher seed rate).
- Total Year 1 incremental: ₹40,000-55,000/acre.
- Payback: typically 1.5-2 seasons.

#### 4.3 Drip Fertigation Schedule

Fertigation by growth stage matches nutrient demand curve more precisely than basal + side-dress.

**Vegetative phase (Days 1-60):**
- N-heavy fertilizer: 19:19:19 NPK at 5 kg/acre/week (5 kg dissolved in tank, injected over 1-2 hours via drip).
- Add humic acid 1 L/acre/week through drip — improves nutrient uptake on calcareous soils.

**Squaring + early flowering (Days 60-90):**
- Balanced + magnesium: 17:44:00 NPK at 3 kg/acre/week + MgSO4 2 kg/acre/week.
- Spray boron (Solubor) 1 g/L foliar at square initiation — critical for boll set.

**Boll development (Days 90-150):**
- K-heavy: 13:00:45 (NPK) at 6 kg/acre/week + 0:52:34 (MAP) at 2 kg/acre/week.
- Micronutrients (Zn + Fe + Mn) chelated mix 500 g/acre every 15 days.
- Flush at 2,000-2,500 L/acre/day minimum (peak demand).

**Boll maturation (Days 150-200):**
- Reduce N to <10% of mix.
- Continue K supply through harvest.
- Slow flush as defoliation approaches.

**Yield uplift vs flood:** 40-60% with HDPS combined with drip fertigation. The two interventions are synergistic — neither alone gets full benefit.

#### 4.4 Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

**Pink bollworm (Pectinophora gossypiella) — primary Bt target, resistance threat**
- Pheromone traps 5/ha (Gossyplure lure), check weekly.
- Action threshold: 8 moths/trap/week.
- ERR (Early Season Reduction of Refuge): not the literal refuge, but practice of removing early-season larvae from infected bolls before they pupate.
- Chemistry: chlorantraniliprole 18.5% SC at 0.4 ml/L OR thiodicarb 75% WP at 1 g/L. Rotate.
- Cultural: timely harvest, burn/bury infested bolls, deep summer plowing to expose pupae.

**Bollworm complex (Helicoverpa armigera, Earias spp.)**
- Bt cotton controls H. armigera adequately, but resistant populations emerging.
- Bt sprays (Bacillus thuringiensis) + HaNPV (Helicoverpa nuclear polyhedrosis virus) at 250 LE/acre.
- Chemistry escalation: spinosad → emamectin benzoate → chlorantraniliprole.
- **Never use synthetic pyrethroids on bollworm** — universal resistance + whitefly flare-up.

**Whitefly (Bemisia tabaci)**
- Massive resistance to imidacloprid in Telangana; avoid.
- Buprofezin 25% SC at 2 ml/L (nymphal IGR) OR spiromesifen 22.9% SC at 1 ml/L.
- Cultural: yellow sticky traps 10/acre; remove sooty mold-affected leaves; avoid alternate hosts (okra near cotton).

**Thrips (Thrips tabaci, Scirtothrips dorsalis)**
- Seedling-stage damage causes "crinkle leaf" syndrome.
- Drenching at sowing: thiamethoxam 25% WG at 4 g/kg seed (seed treatment) OR drench around seed at germination.
- Foliar: fipronil 5% SC at 1 ml/L at first true leaf.

**Jassids (Amrasca biguttula biguttula)**
- "Hopper burn" — leaf margin yellowing then bronzing.
- Neem-based formulations (Azadirachtin 5%) early; escalate to dimethoate 30% EC at 2 ml/L if severe.
- Resistant varieties (hairy leaf types) reduce pressure.

**Mealybug (Phenacoccus solenopsis)**
- Stem and leaf-axil colonies; honeydew + sooty mold.
- Profenofos 50% EC at 2 ml/L OR buprofezin 25% SC at 2 ml/L.
- Encourage natural enemy Cryptolaemus montrouzieri (mealybug ladybird).

#### 4.5 Harvest

Cotton in Telangana is harvested in 3 picks. Mechanical harvest is emerging but still <10% of acreage.

**3-pick schedule:**
- **First pick:** 150-165 days after sowing. ~30% of yield.
- **Second pick:** 165-185 days. ~50% of yield (peak).
- **Third pick:** 185-210 days. ~20% of yield.

**Defoliation (for mechanical harvest):**
- Ethephon 39% SC at 1.5 L/acre, 10 days before planned harvest.
- Triggers leaf abscission; cotton lint quality preserved.
- Required for any cotton picker / stripper; preferred even for manual to reduce trash content.

**Storage and ginning:**
- Sun-dry to <10% moisture before storage.
- Mandi sale within 30 days of harvest preferable (price decay + storage pest risk).
- Direct to ginning factory contracts (e.g., Vimal, Maral Overseas, local ginners) often give ₹100-200/q premium over mandi.

#### 4.6 Yield Benchmarks

| Management level | Yield (q/acre) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Rainfed, conventional spacing, Bt seed only | 8-12 | Default Telangana rainfed |
| Drip + conventional spacing + Bt | 12-16 | Mid-tier irrigated |
| HDPS + drip + Bt + IPM | 18-24 | Progressive farmer |
| HDPS + drip + full IPM + premium varieties | 22-28 | Best case (top decile) |

**Economic worked example (HDPS, drip, 5-acre block):**
- Yield: 22 q/acre × 5 acres = 110 q.
- Price (medium staple): ₹6,800/q × 110 = ₹7.48 L gross.
- Costs (incremental over conventional): drip amortized ₹6,000/acre + extra seed ₹2,500 + IPM ₹5,000 + fertigation ₹8,000 + labor ₹7,000 = ₹28,500/acre × 5 = ₹1.43 L.
- Net incremental over conventional: ~₹4 L per season above conventional baseline.

### 5. Eucalyptus (Yield Optimizer deep-dive)

Eucalyptus is a fast-growing plantation tree, dominant in Telangana's commercial timber-and-pulpwood economy. Unlike annual crops, eucalyptus is a **multi-year commitment** (4-5 years for pulp, 7-8 years for poles, 15+ years for saw timber) with **coppice rotations** that compound the investment.

This sub-section is NEW to the KB — eucalyptus has not been previously covered. It should be treated as a primary section.

#### 5.1 Introduction and Market Positioning

Eucalyptus (Eucalyptus camaldulensis, E. tereticornis, E. grandis, and clonal hybrids) is a fast-growing tree species native to Australia, naturalized across India since the 1950s. In Telangana and AP, eucalyptus is a major plantation crop for:

- **Pulp wood** — paper / rayon industry feedstock; 4-5 year rotation.
- **Poles** — construction scaffolding, agricultural fencing, electric poles; 7-8 year rotation.
- **Saw timber** — pallets, packing crates, low-grade furniture; 12-15+ years.
- **Fuel wood** — local sale, brick-kiln fuel; minor segment.

**Dominant buyers:**
- **ITC Bhadrachalam (ITC PSPD)** — the largest pulp buyer in AP/Telangana. Operates extensive contract-farming network. Prefers clone 1316 (Godavari Delta clone, ITC-bred). Offers technical support + advance + clone supply.
- **JK Paper (Rayagada, Odisha)** — second-largest buyer; sources from northern AP + Telangana borders. Prefers clone 526. Slightly lower price than ITC.
- **APPM (Andhra Pradesh Paper Mills, Rajahmundry)** — historical buyer; mostly spot-market.
- **Local pole traders** — construction industry; pay cash, no advance, no contract; suitable for small holdings (<5 acres).
- **Brick kiln fuel buyers** — last-resort lowest price; rarely the planned exit.

**Yield baseline:** 100-150 tons/acre over a 5-year rotation under good clone selection + management. Wide variability (40-200 t/acre observed) driven by clone × soil × water.

**Coppice cycles:**
- Rotation 1 (planting → year 5): baseline yield.
- Rotation 2 (year 5 → year 10, coppice): ~85% of rotation 1.
- Rotation 3 (year 10 → year 15, coppice): ~70% of rotation 1.
- Beyond rotation 3, stump mortality and yield decline force replanting decision.

#### 5.2 Clone Selection (Critical — More Important Than "Variety")

In eucalyptus, "variety" is a less meaningful term than "clone" — most commercial planting is vegetatively propagated clonal material. Clone selection is the single most important decision affecting 5-year outcomes.

**Clone 1316 (ITC clone, Godavari Delta origin)**
- Best fit for Telangana conditions + ITC Bhadrachalam buyer relationship.
- MAI (Mean Annual Increment): 30-35 m³/acre/year — high productivity.
- Moderate drought tolerance.
- Preferred for Jangaon, Suryapet, Khammam black cotton soils.
- Source: ITC Bhadrachalam nursery (free or subsidized to contract growers); State Forest Dept nurseries.

**Clone 526 (JK Paper clone)**
- JK Paper preferred; slightly lower MAI than 1316 but better tree form (straighter, fewer forks).
- Good performance in red lateritic soils.
- 25-30 m³/acre/year MAI.

**Clone 411**
- Bred for drought tolerance.
- Suited to drier areas (Mahbubnagar, parts of Wanaparthy, drier Khammam).
- Lower yield ceiling (20-25 m³/acre/year) but much lower risk of crop failure.

**Clone 71, Clone 7 (older clones)**
- Earlier-generation material; performance now eclipsed by 1316 and 526.
- Mentioned only because some old nurseries still distribute these.

**Sourcing discipline:**
- ITC Bhadrachalam nursery — free or subsidized for contract growers.
- State Forest Dept nurseries (TS Forest Dept, AP Forest Dept) — certified material, low cost.
- Private certified nurseries (IFGTB-certified, ICFRE-certified).
- **AVOID uncertified roadside / unregistered nurseries** — performance highly variable, often hybrid swarm of unknown lineage, yield can drop 40-50%.

#### 5.3 Land Preparation

**Site assessment first:**
- Avoid waterlogged sites (eucalyptus is intolerant of standing water despite high water use).
- Black cotton vertisols: feasible but require raised-bed / mound planting.
- Red soils: ideal.
- Lateritic / gravelly: acceptable with deeper pits.
- Saline / alkaline (pH > 8.5): avoid or use specific tolerant clones (411 has some tolerance).

**Land prep operations:**
1. **Deep plowing 2-3 times** in summer (April-May) — breaks subsoil compaction.
2. **Cross-plowing** at 90° to first pass.
3. **Pit digging** — 45 × 45 × 45 cm pits at spacing per density target (see 5.4).
4. **Pit filling:**
   - 5 kg FYM (Farm Yard Manure) per pit (well-decomposed cattle manure).
   - 250 g Single Super Phosphate (SSP).
   - 500 g neem cake (termite + nematode deterrent).
   - Topsoil from pit excavation mixed with the above.
5. **Pre-monsoon planting:** June-July ideal (first monsoon rains drive establishment).
6. **Black cotton soils:** raised-bed (30 cm above natural level) or mound planting (50 × 50 × 30 cm mound) prevents waterlogging stress.

#### 5.4 Spacing and Planting Density

| Purpose | Spacing | Plants/acre |
|---|---|---|
| Pulpwood (5-year rotation) | 2 m × 2 m | 1,000 |
| Pulpwood (intensive) | 2 m × 1.5 m | 1,333 |
| Pulpwood (ultra-intensive) | 2 m × 1 m | 2,000 |
| Pole / timber | 3 m × 3 m | 444 |
| Pole (alternate spacing) | 2.5 m × 2.5 m | 640 |
| Mixed (planted dense, thinned) | 2 m × 1 m initial → 4 m × 2 m post-thin | 2,000 → 500 |

Note: "plants/acre" numbers above use 4,047 m²/acre. Some Telangana references quote higher densities by treating 1 acre as ~5,000 m² — verify field measurements.

**Density logic:**
- Higher density → faster canopy closure → less weeding → smaller individual tree but more total wood.
- Lower density → larger trees → suitable for pole/timber market.
- Intensive (2 × 1 m) with thinning at year 2 is a hedge — gives wood from thinned trees + larger residual.

#### 5.5 Water Requirements

Eucalyptus is a high-water-consumption crop — a single rotation can transpire 12-15 ML/acre over 5 years.

**Implications:**
- Suryapet, Nalgonda bore-well regions: first rotation fine; second rotation often shows water table decline (10-20 ft drop reported in some clusters).
- Telangana government has issued periodic restrictions on eucalyptus in water-stressed mandals (see 5.10).
- KVK Suryapet caution: **assess water table depth + bore-well age before committing > 5-acre plantations**.

**Irrigation schedule:**
- **Year 1 (establishment):** Drip irrigation strongly recommended. 4-6 L/plant/day in dry months. Without drip: 1 bucket (5 L) per plant weekly in summer.
- **Year 2:** Drip reduces to 2 L/plant/day. By end of Year 2, trees mostly tap subsurface moisture.
- **Year 3+:** Mostly rain-fed; supplemental irrigation only in severe drought.

**Drip economics:**
- Year 1 drip system: ₹15,000-25,000/acre (lighter spec than vegetable drip).
- Pays back in survival rate alone (saves 15-25% mortality in dry establishment year).

#### 5.6 Coppice Management

The single most important post-establishment economic lever.

**Coppice biology:**
- After first harvest, the stump produces multiple shoots from dormant buds at root collar.
- These shoots grow much faster than seedlings (existing root system + carbohydrate reserves).
- 5-year rotation 2 yield: ~85% of rotation 1, achieved with ~30% of the cost.

**Coppice protocol:**
1. **First harvest (year 4-5):** Cut stems 15-30 cm above ground. Use sharp axe or chainsaw — clean cut prevents bark splitting.
2. **Cutting season:** November-January (cool, dry — minimizes pathogen entry into cut surface).
3. **Stump treatment:** Optional — paint cut surface with copper oxychloride paste to prevent stump rot.
4. **Coppice shoot management:**
   - At 3-6 months: select 2-3 best (most vigorous, straightest) shoots per stump; remove all others.
   - At 12 months: thin to 1-2 final shoots per stump (single shoot for pole / timber; 2 shoots for pulpwood).
5. **Second rotation harvest:** Year 9-10 (4-5 year cycle from coppice).
6. **Third rotation:** Year 13-15 (coppice from second rotation stumps).

**Stump mortality:**
- ~10-15% by rotation 2 (some stumps fail to coppice).
- ~25-30% by rotation 3.
- Gap-fill with new clonal plants at coppice initiation.

**Total productive life:**
- 3 rotations × 4-5 years = 12-15 years on a single planting.
- After rotation 3: declining yield + stump decay → replant.

#### 5.7 Gall Wasp (Leptocybe invasa) Management

Gall wasp is the single most damaging eucalyptus pest in AP/Telangana since 2008 (when it was first reported in India). Devastating if not managed.

**Symptoms:**
- Bumpy galls on leaf petioles, midribs, and young shoots.
- Tip death of growing shoots.
- Stunted growth; severe infestation kills young trees.
- All ages affected, but 1-3 year-old trees most vulnerable.

**Control:**
- **Biocontrol — Quadrastichus mendeli parasitoid:**
  - Released by Forest Dept / IFGTB in many AP/Telangana clusters.
  - Request supply from KVK Suryapet, IFGTB Coimbatore, or local Forest Dept nursery.
  - Establish in plantation by releasing 200-500 wasps/acre at first gall sighting.
  - Self-sustaining once established — no further intervention needed.
- **Cultural:** Remove + burn heavily infested tips during routine maintenance pruning.
- **Clone selection:** Avoid highly susceptible clones — check IFGTB clone resistance table. Clone 1316 has moderate resistance; clones 71 and 7 (old) are highly susceptible.
- **Chemical (last resort):** Imidacloprid soil drench at 1 ml/plant in Year 1-2; rarely cost-effective in pulp economics, but used in pole / timber plantings where individual tree value is higher.

#### 5.8 Allelopathy

Eucalyptus leaf litter and root exudates contain phenolic compounds that suppress understorey vegetation. Practical implications:

- **Intercropping window:** First 12-18 months only, before canopy closure. Suitable intercrops: cowpea, blackgram, sesame, groundnut (short-duration legumes).
- After canopy closure: nearly bare orchard floor; nothing grows well.
- **Avoid placement near sensitive systems:** vegetable gardens, paddy fields downslope of plantation, sensitive horticulture (papaya, banana).
- **Soil legacy:** Allelopathic effects persist 1-2 years after plantation removal. Wait 2 years before planting sensitive successor crops.

#### 5.9 Carbon Credit Potential

Eucalyptus plantations qualify for afforestation/reforestation (A/R) carbon credits under:
- VCS (Verra Carbon Standard).
- Gold Standard.
- India-specific (emerging): Green Credit Programme (Government of India).

**Sequestration estimate:**
- 5-8 tCO2e/acre/year, depending on rotation length, clone, and management.
- Longer rotations (pole/timber) sequester more per year than short pulpwood rotations.

**Revenue:**
- Current voluntary carbon market: ₹400-1,200/tCO2e (highly variable, often ₹500-800).
- Per-acre revenue: ₹2,000-5,000/acre/year if credits monetized.

**Caveats:**
- Certification process takes 2-3 years (baseline survey + monitoring + verification).
- Upfront certification cost: ₹15,000-25,000/acre.
- Aggregator share: typically 30-50% of credit revenue.
- Viable only at scale: >10 acres minimum, >25 acres preferred.
- NABARD has run pilot schemes 2023-2025 to onboard small farmers via FPO aggregation.

#### 5.10 Buyer Contract Terms (Detailed)

**ITC Bhadrachalam:**
- Price: ₹4,500-5,500/ton green wood (varies by year + grade + moisture content).
- Advance: ₹15,000-25,000/acre, repaid from first harvest payment.
- 5-year supply agreement.
- Free clone supply (subsidized otherwise) + technical support visits (3-4/year).
- Pickup logistics: ITC arranges transport from designated collection points.
- Penalty clauses: under-supply (< 80% of contracted) triggers advance recovery + 10% penalty.

**JK Paper (Rayagada):**
- Price: ₹4,200-5,200/ton (slightly below ITC).
- Advance: ₹10,000-20,000/acre.
- 5-year agreement.
- Technical support via field extension officers.
- Better fit for farms in Bhadradri, Khammam border districts (closer mill).

**APPM (Andhra Pradesh Paper Mills):**
- Spot price (no advance, no contract).
- More flexible but more risky.
- Price floats with pulp market — can be lower OR higher than contract buyers.
- Suitable for farmers without urgent cash needs who can wait for price spikes.

**Local pole traders:**
- Cash payment at farm gate.
- ₹6,000-9,000/ton for poles (significantly above pulp price).
- Suitable for: < 5 acre holdings; pole-quality material (straight, ≥ 25 cm girth at breast height).
- Buyer concentration risk: 2-3 local traders per district, can collude on price.

**Decision framework:**
- > 10 acres + need predictable cash flow → ITC or JK contract.
- < 5 acres or quality pole material → local trader.
- Risk-tolerant + market-savvy → APPM spot market.

#### 5.11 Water Regulation Caution (Telangana / AP)

**Telangana GO Ms 69 (2007)** + subsequent state orders:
- Restrict eucalyptus planting within 500 m of perennial water bodies (lakes, rivers, irrigation channels).
- Restrict in certain drought-prone mandals (notified list updated periodically by Forest Dept).
- Local panchayats may have additional restrictions (sometimes more, sometimes less strict).

**Compliance checklist before planting:**
1. Check with local Revenue office (Tahsildar) — is the mandal on the restricted list?
2. Check with Forest Dept ranger — any pending restriction notification?
3. Check with Panchayat — local resolutions on eucalyptus?
4. Measure distance to nearest water body (lake, tank, channel, well).

**Non-compliance risk:**
- Forest Dept can order felling without compensation (rarely enforced, but precedent exists).
- Bank loans (KCC, term loan) may be denied for prohibited plantations.

#### 5.12 Economics Summary

**Year 1 (establishment):**
- Land prep + pit digging: ₹4,000-6,000/acre.
- Clonal plants (1,000-1,500 plants at ₹6-10/plant): ₹6,000-15,000/acre.
- FYM + fertilizer + neem cake: ₹3,000-5,000/acre.
- Drip irrigation (Year 1): ₹15,000-25,000/acre.
- Planting labor + Year 1 weeding + irrigation labor: ₹3,000-5,000/acre.
- **Total Year 1: ₹25,000-35,000/acre** (with drip).

**Years 2-4 (maintenance):**
- Weeding (decreasing over time): ₹1,500-3,000/acre/year.
- Pest monitoring + occasional Quadrastichus release: ₹500-1,000/acre/year.
- Maintenance labor: ₹2,000-3,000/acre/year.
- **Total Years 2-4: ₹4,000-6,000/acre/year × 3 years = ₹12,000-18,000/acre.**

**Year 5 (harvest):**
- Felling + loading + transport (often buyer-arranged): ₹500-1,000/ton.
- Net to farmer: ₹3,500-5,000/ton (after harvest costs).
- **Gross revenue:** 100 tons/acre × ₹4,500/ton = ₹4.5 L/acre (high case) to ₹3.5 L (typical).
- **Less harvest costs + advance recovery:** ₹1.5-2 L net cash.

**5-year cumulative:**
- Investment: ₹40,000-55,000/acre.
- Gross harvest: ₹3.5-5 L/acre.
- Net: ₹50,000-80,000/acre over 5 years (₹10,000-16,000/acre/year — modest, but compounds with coppice).

**Coppice rotations (much cheaper):**
- Investment: ₹5,000-8,000/acre maintenance only.
- Yield: 85% (rotation 2), 70% (rotation 3) of original.
- Net per coppice cycle: ₹2.5-4 L/acre.
- 15-year total net (across 3 rotations): ₹7-12 L/acre.

|---|---|---|
| Poor clones (uncertified) + poor management | 60-80 | Common in unmanaged plantings |
| Good clones + minimal management | 80-100 | Typical |
| Good clones + good management (Year 1 drip, IPM) | 100-140 | Progressive farmers |

### 6. Palm Oil (Yield Optimizer deep-dive)

Oil palm (Elaeis guineensis) is a 25-30 year productive perennial crop, with the longest commitment of any crop in the Yield Optimizer scope. The National Mission on Edible Oils — Oil Palm (NMEO-OP), launched 2021, has created significant policy + subsidy + buyback infrastructure to expand oil palm in India, with Telangana (Khammam, Bhadradri, parts of Nalgonda/Suryapet) and AP (West Godavari, East Godavari, Krishna) as key target states.

This sub-section is NEW to the KB — oil palm has not been previously covered. The Yield Optimizer must cover seedling selection, water management (oil palm is highly water-demanding), pollination management (a specific introduced weevil), Ganoderma rot prevention (the asset-destroying disease), buyback contract selection, and the NMEO-OP subsidy structure.

#### 6.1 Introduction and Policy Context

**Oil palm basics:**
- Species: Elaeis guineensis (African oil palm) — the commercially dominant species globally.
- Productive life: 25-30 years (replanting at year 25-30).
- Establishment: 4 years from planting to first commercial harvest.
- Peak yield: years 7-15.
- Decline: years 20+ (harvest becomes difficult as trees reach 12-15 m height).
- Continuous year-round harvest (10-12 day cycle) once mature.

**NMEO-OP (National Mission on Edible Oils — Oil Palm):**
- Launched 2021 by Government of India to reduce edible oil imports (currently ~60% of demand imported).
- Targets 10 lakh hectares of new oil palm by 2025-26.
- Subsidy structure (per hectare):
  - **Year 1:** ₹29,000 (plant material + infrastructure: drip + pump + fencing).
  - **Year 2:** ₹7,500 (maintenance + fertilizer support).
  - **Year 3:** ₹6,000 (maintenance + fertilizer).
  - **Pest management support:** ₹2,000/ha additional.
- **Price Assurance Mechanism:** Government-backed minimum price of ₹7,000/quintal FFB (Fresh Fruit Bunch). If market price falls below, government compensates difference.
- Subsidy delivery via: District Agriculture Officer → Horticulture Department.

**Telangana / AP suitability:**
- Khammam, Bhadradri-Kothagudem — primary expansion zone.
- West Godavari, East Godavari, Krishna (AP) — established oil palm belt since 1990s.
- Suryapet, Nalgonda alluvial tracts — feasible with adequate water.
- NOT suitable: drought-prone mandals (Mahbubnagar, Wanaparthy), saline soils, heavy black cotton without drainage.

#### 6.2 DxP Hybrid Selection (Dura × Pisifera)

Commercial oil palm is exclusively the DxP hybrid — Dura (thick shell, low oil) crossed with Pisifera (no shell, sterile female) produces Tenera offspring (thin shell, high oil, fertile). All Tenera planting material must come from controlled cross of certified Dura × Pisifera parents.

**Murugappa Group DxP (3F Industries):**
- Most widely planted hybrid in AP/Telangana.
- Yield: 4-6 t FFB/acre/year at maturity.
- Source: TATA-certified nurseries (Hyderabad, Suryapet, Khammam dealers).
- Pricing: ₹250-350/seedling.
- Recommended for most Telangana plantings.

**ICAR-IIOPR Pedavegi (Andhra Pradesh) hybrid material:**
- Research-station-bred high-yield material.
- Yield: 5-7 t FFB/acre/year at maturity.
- Source: ICAR-IIOPR Pedavegi research station (limited availability; book 6-9 months ahead).
- Pricing: ₹200-300/seedling.
- Slower commercial supply but premium genetics.

**Lipsa, Godrej Agrovet, and other DxP sources:**
- Multiple commercial nurseries supply DxP in AP/Telangana.
- All should provide PCO (Plant Certification Officer) certification.

**Hybrid criteria:**
- **MANDATORY:** Certified seedlings only. Roadside / unregistered "oil palm plants" are often Dura (parent) or unproven crosses — yield can be 30-50% of expected.
- Look for PCO stamp on each pot.
- Insist on documentation of parent cross + nursery batch.
- Reject seedlings older than 12 months in pot (rootbound; underperform).
- Reject seedlings younger than 9 months (insufficient root development for transplant survival).

#### 6.3 Planting Geometry

**Triangular packing (preferred):**
- 9 m × 9 m equilateral triangle = 160 plants/acre.
- Triangular packing gives 15-20% more plants per unit area vs rectangular.
- Best light interception and canopy structure.

**Rectangular planting:**
- 9 m × 7.8 m = 143 plants/acre.
- Simpler to lay out, mechanize.

**Pit preparation:**
- Pit size: 60 × 60 × 60 cm (large pits — palms are heavy feeders).
- Pit filling:
  - FYM 25 kg/pit (well-decomposed; oil palm is heavy organic-matter feeder).
  - Rock phosphate 500 g/pit (slow-release P).
  - Neem cake 1 kg/pit (termite + nematode deterrent).
  - Topsoil layered with the above.
- Pits dug 4-6 weeks before planting to allow weathering.

**Planting season:**
- June-September preferred (monsoon establishment).
- Avoid hot summer planting (April-May) unless heavy irrigation guaranteed.

#### 6.4 Water Requirements

Oil palm is one of the most water-demanding crops grown in India. **Drip irrigation is mandatory** — flood-irrigated oil palm fails commercially.

**Daily water requirement (per mature tree):**
- Hot dry months (March-May): 200-250 L/tree/day.
- Wet months (July-October): 100-150 L/tree/day (supplemented by rain).
- Cool dry months (December-February): 150-200 L/tree/day.

**Annual total:** ~50,000-70,000 L/tree/year × 160 trees/acre = 8-11 ML/acre/year.

**Drip system specifications:**
- 4-8 drippers per tree (1-2 L/h each).
- Daily irrigation schedule: 2 hours minimum.
- Pressure-compensating drippers preferred (uniform across hectare).
- Automatic timer + valve recommended (manual operation prone to error in year-round cropping).

**Fertigation:**
- K is the primary nutrient (palm fruit bunches are K-heavy).
- K2O annual requirement (mature tree): 5-7 kg per tree per year = 800-1,100 kg/acre/year.
- Fertigation schedule: weekly application of 12:61:0 (vegetative phase) shifting to 13:0:45 (productive phase).
- Mg, B, Cu are critical micronutrients (deficiencies common in Indian soils).

**Consequences of inadequate water:**
- Fruit abortion (developing bunches turn black, drop prematurely).
- Reduced bunch number (palm "decides" to drop floral initials when stressed).
- 50%+ yield loss in drought-affected palms.
- Severe stress can cause palm death (bole rot follows water stress).

| Year | Stage | Yield (t FFB/acre) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | Establishment | 0 | Vegetative growth only |
| 3 | First flowers | 0.5-1 | Remove early bunches to channel energy to vegetative frame |
| 4 | First commercial harvest | 1-2 | Small bunches |

#### 6.6 Year-Round Harvest Cycle

Unlike annual crops, oil palm harvest is **continuous**, year-round:

**Harvest mechanics:**
- Bunches mature on a 10-12 day cycle per palm — different palms at different stages on any given day.
- Harvest crew visits each palm every 10-12 days.
- Mature bunches identified by: 5+ loose fruits dropped at base, bunch color shift to orange-red.
- Climbing or pole-cutting (for tall palms) used to cut bunches.

**Critical: bunches must reach mill within 24-48 hours.**
- After cutting, FFA (Free Fatty Acid) content rises rapidly.
- Mill rejection threshold typically FFA > 5% (some mills 3.5%).
- High FFA = price cuts or rejection.

**Mill distance constraint:**
- **Maximum economic mill distance: 50-80 km.**
- Beyond 80 km: logistics cost + FFA risk make oil palm uneconomic.
- This single factor determines whether oil palm is even viable for a given farm location.

**Labor:**
- Year-round harvest crew (1 person can handle ~5 acres at peak).
- No "off-season" — even during monsoon and festivals.
- Labor availability is a real planning constraint (Telangana rural labor migration impacts year-round operations).

#### 6.7 Weevil Pollination Management (Critical)

Oil palm pollination is dominated by **Elaeidobius kamerunicus** (African oil palm weevil), introduced to India for palm pollination.

**Background:**
- Released by State Agriculture Department in many AP/Telangana districts since the 1990s.
- Now naturalized in most established oil palm areas.
- Without weevil: pollination drops to 10-30%; fruit set very poor; yield collapse.

**Critical management rule:**
- **NO blanket insecticide sprays during male flower anthesis.**
- Synthetic pyrethroids and organophosphates wipe out weevil populations.
- A single ill-timed pyrethroid spray can crash the local weevil population for 6-12 months → 50-70% yield loss in subsequent flush.

**Targeted application protocol:**
- For Rhinoceros beetle (only known pest requiring chemical intervention): apply carbaryl OR chlorpyrifos GRANULES into leaf axils, NOT foliar spray.
- Remove decaying organic matter (Rhinoceros beetle breeding sites) — old fronds, decaying boles.
- Other pests (red palm weevil, scale): pheromone trapping or targeted treatments only.

**Weevil monitoring:**
- Visual count of weevils on male inflorescences (10-20 weevils per inflorescence = adequate).
- If count drops to <5/inflorescence: investigate (recent spray? drought? mass mortality?).
- Re-release weevils from established plantings if local population collapses (sourceable from AP Horticulture Dept).

#### 6.8 Ganoderma (Basal Stem Rot) Prevention

Ganoderma boninense is the single most asset-destroying disease in oil palm globally. No cure once established. Multi-decade plantation can be destroyed.

**Pathogenesis:**
- Soil-borne white-rot fungus.
- Colonizes palm root system, advances into bole, hollows out base.
- Tree appears healthy for years, then suddenly leans / topples.
- High-incidence areas: heavy clay soils, poor drainage, areas previously planted to oil palm or coconut (Ganoderma is shared host).

**Early indicators:**
- Yellowing of oldest (lowest) leaves while younger leaves still green.
- Reduced bunch production over 2-3 seasons.
- Bracket fungi (orange-brown shelf-like structures) at base of palm.
- Soft, hollow sound when base of palm is struck.

**Prevention (the ONLY strategy that works):**
1. **Site selection:** Plant on well-drained soils. Avoid waterlogged sites.
2. **Avoid burying palm debris:** Lignin in palm waste feeds Ganoderma. Burn or remove decaying fronds, boles, root systems.
3. **Certified disease-free seedlings:** Insist on Ganoderma-free nursery source.
4. **Avoid replanting through old palm stumps:** Remove and destroy old stumps completely (excavate root mass).
5. **Quarantine zones around any infected palm:** 5 m exclusion zone; do not plant new palms within.

**Sanitation when infection detected:**
- Remove infected palm + entire root system + 2 m radius of soil.
- Burn debris on-site (do not transport to other parts of plantation).
- Solarize the soil for 60 days (clear plastic sheeting in hot season).
- Apply hexaconazole soil drench (5 g/L, 20 L per pit) to adjacent palms within 5 m.
- Do not replant in infected pit for 2-3 years; if replanted, use trichoderma soil enrichment (5 kg per pit).

**Long-term outlook:**
- Once Ganoderma is established in a plantation, gradual yield decline is inevitable over 10-15 years.
- Most Malaysian/Indonesian plantations face Ganoderma by replant age — implication for India: be vigilant from the start.

#### 6.9 Buyback Contract Strategy (Detailed)

Oil palm is unusual in that the buyer contract often determines viability more than agronomy. FFB has no mandi market — there is no "spot sale to whoever shows up." The buyer / mill relationship is the foundation.

**Patanjali (Ruchi Soya):**
- Active in Telangana since acquisition of Ruchi Soya.
- 25-year contract.
- Price: ₹7,000-8,000/quintal FFB (varies by mill efficiency, year).
- Advance: ₹25,000-40,000/acre.
- Recovery: 5-7% deduction from each FFB payment until advance repaid.
- Strong technical extension service.
- Mill: Khammam region.

**Godrej Agrovet:**
- AP/Telangana operations from mid-2000s.
- Price: ₹7,200-8,500/quintal FFB.
- Advance: ₹20,000-35,000/acre.
- Stricter grading — FFA% limit 3.5% (vs Patanjali ~5%).
- Strong technical support; field officers visit monthly.
- Mill: West Godavari + Khammam.

**3F Industries (Foods, Fats & Fertilizers):**
- AP-based; similar terms to Godrej.
- Good logistics in coastal AP.
- Price competitive; FFA grading moderate.
- Mill: Eluru (West Godavari).

**APFPCO (AP Foods Producers' Cooperative):**
- Government cooperative buyer.
- Guaranteed floor price ₹7,000/quintal (NMEO-OP minimum).
- No advance.
- Good for risk-averse farmers, those with own capital.
- Lower technical support than corporate buyers.

**Selection criteria (in priority order):**
1. **Mill distance** (< 60 km mandatory; < 40 km preferred).
2. **Advance availability** (especially Year 1-3 cash flow critical).
3. **Technical support quality** (visits, agronomy guidance, NMEO-OP documentation help).
4. **FFA grading penalty clauses** (>4% FFA attracts 5-15% price cut; >5% may trigger rejection).
5. **Contract flexibility** (can you exit at year 10? penalty?).
6. **Pricing transparency** (formula-based vs discretionary).

**Common contract pitfalls:**
- Advance recovery rate too high (>10% per harvest = cash flow strain).
- FFA grading subjective (insist on lab-tested, not visual).
- "Force majeure" clauses overly broad (allow buyer to refuse pickup in mill maintenance).
- Lock-in penalty too high (exit fees >₹50,000/acre kills flexibility).

#### 6.10 NMEO-OP Subsidy Details

**Subsidy structure (per hectare, current 2024-2026 cycle):**
- Year 1: ₹29,000 (plant material + infrastructure: drip system + pumping + fencing).
- Year 2: ₹7,500 (maintenance + fertilizer support).
- Year 3: ₹6,000 (maintenance + fertilizer).
- Pest management support: ₹2,000/ha additional (one-time during establishment).
- Replanting eligibility: After 25 years (palm lifecycle end).

**Application process:**
1. **Eligibility documentation:**
   - Land patta copy (proof of ownership or long-term lease).
   - Aadhar card.
   - Bank account details (subsidy is DBT — direct benefit transfer).
   - Land measurement sketch (showing acreage + boundary).
2. **Application through:** District Agriculture Officer → Horticulture Department.
3. **Field verification:** Horticulture Dept officer visits site, verifies measurements + soil/water suitability.
4. **Sanction order:** Issued within 60-90 days typically (faster in established oil palm districts like Khammam, slower in new expansion zones).
5. **Subsidy release:** First tranche on completion of planting (verified by field officer); subsequent tranches on annual maintenance inspection.

**Documentation discipline:**
- Maintain field diary (planting date, drip installation date, fertilizer applications, harvest records).
- Required for subsequent year subsidy tranches.
- Required for replanting subsidy after 25 years.

| Risk | Severity | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Ganoderma rot | Catastrophic (irreversible) | Site selection, sanitation, certified seedlings |
| Buyer contract lock-in | High (25-year contract) | Compare 2-3 buyers before signing; visit other contracted farmers |
| Water shortage (bore well depletion) | High (palm dies under prolonged drought) | Bore-well depth assessment; backup water source |

| Pollinator weevil collapse | Medium (yield crash) | Strict no-pyrethroid policy; monitor weevil counts |

#### 6.12 Economics (Detailed)

**Year 1:**
- Seedlings: 160 × ₹300 = ₹48,000/acre.
- Drip installation: ₹25,000-35,000/acre.
- Pit preparation + FYM + neem cake + rock phosphate: ₹8,000/acre.
- Planting labor + Year 1 irrigation + weeding: ₹5,000/acre.
- **Total Year 1: ₹86,000-1,00,000/acre gross**, BEFORE NMEO-OP subsidy.
- After NMEO-OP Year 1 subsidy (₹29,000/ha = ~₹11,700/acre): **net Year 1: ₹70,000-90,000/acre**.

**Year 2-3:**
- Fertilizer + fertigation: ₹15,000-20,000/acre/year.
- Maintenance labor: ₹5,000-8,000/acre/year.
- Water + irrigation power: ₹5,000-8,000/acre/year.
- **Total Year 2-3: ₹25,000-35,000/acre/year.**
- After NMEO-OP Year 2 (~₹3,000/acre) and Year 3 (~₹2,400/acre): net ₹22,000-32,000/acre/year.

**Year 4 onwards:**
- Revenue starts: ~1-2 t FFB/acre × ₹7,500/q = ₹7,500-15,000/acre.
- Costs continue: ₹25,000-35,000/acre/year.
- Net: still negative ₹10,000-20,000/acre.

**Year 7+ (peak production):**
- Yield: 7 t FFB/acre × ₹7,500/q = ₹52,500/acre gross.
- Costs: ₹20,000-25,000/acre (fertilizer, labor, water).
- **Net: ₹30,000-45,000/acre/year.**

**Break-even:** Typically Year 5-6 cumulative (with NMEO-OP subsidy) or Year 7-8 without.

**25-year NPV (10% discount):** Positive from Year 8 onwards in typical case; significantly improved by NMEO-OP subsidy front-loading cash flow.

**Sensitivity factors:**
- Mill distance > 60 km: NPV drops 15-25%.
- Yield 5 t/acre instead of 7 t/acre: NPV drops 30-40%.
- Ganoderma infection at Year 10: NPV drops 50%+ (early replanting required).
- Buyer price stuck at ₹7,000 floor (no upside): NPV drops 10-15%.

| Good management (drip, fertigation, IPM) | 5-7 | Most established farms |

## Cross-Crop Yield Optimizer Notes

### Common High-Leverage Interventions Across All Crops

1. **Soil test before planning:** ₹500-1,000 per sample saves 5-15% of fertilizer cost and corrects pH / micronutrient blind spots. Re-test every 2-3 years for annuals, every 5 years for perennials.

2. **Drip irrigation as the foundation:** Across paddy (DSR), cotton (HDPS), mango (off-season), eucalyptus (Year 1), and oil palm (mandatory) — drip is the single intervention that pays for itself fastest. Investment ₹25,000-50,000/acre, recovered in 1-3 seasons typically.

3. **IPM as the loss-prevention foundation:** Pheromone monitoring + threshold-based spraying + biocontrol agents systematically beats calendar-based blanket spraying on cost, yield, and resistance management. Initial setup (traps, supplies) ₹2,000-5,000/acre.

4. **Nitrogen splits:** Across paddy, maize, cotton, oil palm — splitting N into 3-5 stage-aligned applications outperforms single basal by 15-30% yield, often with the same total N.

5. **Buyer contract clarity:** Across high-investment perennials (mango, eucalyptus, oil palm) — the buyer contract (or absence) determines whether good agronomy translates to good economics. Signed contract with reputable buyer is often worth more than 10% yield uplift.

6. **Labor planning:** Year-round crops (oil palm) and labor-intensive systems (SRI paddy, HDPS cotton) succeed or fail based on labor availability. Plan labor model BEFORE planting.

### Crop-Specific Yield Optimizer Decision Triggers

The Yield Optimizer module should escalate to **human review (KVK officer, district horticulture officer, agricultural consultant)** when:

- **Paddy:** Switch from continuous flood to SRI on >5 acres (labor + transplanter logistics).
- **Mango:** First PBZ application on a commercial orchard (irreversible dose mistakes).
- **Maize:** First-year FAW management in a previously unaffected farm (cultivar + chemistry choice).
- **Cotton:** Switch to HDPS on >3 acres (drip + variety + IPM coordination).
- **Eucalyptus:** Any planting > 5 acres (water table assessment, regulatory check, buyer contract).
- **Oil palm:** ANY oil palm planting decision (25-year commitment; mill distance + contract critical).

### A. Paddy Extended Diagnostics

#### A.1 Symptom-to-Cause Lookup

| Symptom | Likely cause | Confirmation step | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Yellowing from leaf tip back | N deficiency (or late N split missed) | LCC score < 3 | Top-dress urea 20-25 kg/acre; spray foliar 2% urea |
| Whole-plant yellowing | Fe deficiency (high pH, calcareous) | Soil pH > 8 + young leaves yellow | Foliar FeSO4 0.5% + soil acidification (sulfur 50 kg/acre) |
| Stunted plants in patches | Zn deficiency (especially after green manure incorporation) | Foliar Zn spray test on small patch — recovery in 7 days = Zn deficiency | Foliar ZnSO4 0.5% twice at 10-day interval |
| Brown spots on leaves with yellow halo | Bacterial leaf blight | Lesion margins linear, water-soaked | Copper oxychloride 2 g/L; drain field; reduce N |
| Diamond-shaped gray-center lesions | Blast (leaf phase) | Magnify — center is gray necrotic | Tricyclazole 0.6 g/L immediately |
| Dead heart at tillering | Stem borer | Pull dead tiller — larva inside | Cartap 4G granules in standing water |

#### C.3 Fall Armyworm Decision Tree

```
Pheromone trap catch > 5/trap/week OR visible damage on >5% plants?
├── NO → Continue monitoring; no spray
└── YES
    ├── Plant stage V2-V6 (whorl present)?
    │   ├── YES → Spray INTO whorl (emamectin or spinosad)
    │   │   ├── Re-monitor 5 days later → If still active, repeat with different chemistry
    │   │   └── If natural enemies (parasitoid wasps, predator bugs) visible → Skip spray, monitor
    │   └── NO (post-tasseling) → Foliar spray largely ineffective; protect cobs with Bt spray
    └── Egg masses visible on leaf undersides?
        ├── YES + small acreage → Hand removal cost-effective
        └── YES + large acreage → Egg parasitoid (Trichogramma) release
```

#### D.2 HDPS Suitability Checklist

A farm is suitable for HDPS only if ALL of these are true:
- ✓ Drip irrigation available or budget for installation (₹35,000-50,000/acre).
- ✓ Reliable water source for daily irrigation (bore well capacity ≥ 1,500 L/h per acre).
- ✓ Power supply 6+ hours/day for irrigation pump.
- ✓ Access to compact/early HDPS-suited variety (Bunny Bt, RCH 2, JK 1947).
- ✓ Labor model for manual spraying with foot-operated power sprayer (NOT bullock-cart sprayer — too wide).
- ✓ Capacity to defoliate with ethephon at harvest (machine harvest OR labor for clean pick).
- ✓ Soil drainage adequate (no waterlogging risk).
- ✗ Pure rainfed without backup irrigation = NOT suitable.
- ✗ Highly mechanized broadacre system (HDPS needs intensive management).

## Yield Optimizer — Soil-Specific Adaptations

Each of the 6 crops responds differently to Telangana's three dominant soil types: **black cotton (vertisol), red soil (alfisol), and alluvial / coastal soil**. The Yield Optimizer must adapt recommendations to soil.

### G. Soil × Crop Adaptation Matrix

| Crop | Black cotton (vertisol) | Red soil (alfisol) | Alluvial / coastal |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Paddy** | Standard transplanted; sometimes K supplementation low (high native K). SRI feasible if puddling adequate. | Higher input requirement; Zn deficiency common. Foliar Zn essential. | Best fit; default Telangana paddy soil. |
| **Mango** | Suitable but pre-monsoon waterlogging risk. Plant on raised mounds (30 cm). Drainage channels needed. | Excellent fit; good drainage; native fertility supports yield. | Acceptable; watch for salinity in coastal areas. |
| **Maize** | Suitable kharif (use moisture). Rabi marginal (cracking deprives roots of water). | Good fit; both kharif + rabi. Sufficient water source needed. | Excellent for kharif + rabi double crop. |
| **Cotton** | EXCELLENT — cotton's preferred soil. Black cotton soils hold moisture well; deep root exploration. | Acceptable but lower yield ceiling than vertisol. Drip + organic matter help. | Suitable; coastal salinity tolerance moderate. |
| **Eucalyptus** | Suitable but raised-bed planting essential. Waterlogging kills first-year saplings. | Good fit; well-drained. Clone 526 preferred. | Suitable if salinity < 2 dS/m. |
| **Oil palm** | Marginal — drainage problems. Need extensive drainage channels. Heavy soil amendment with organic matter. | Good fit; well-drained. Preferred for NMEO-OP expansion. | Excellent — coastal AP belt (West Godavari) shows this. |

### H. Crop-Crop Rotation Compatibility

| Previous crop | Best follow crop | Crop to AVOID following | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Paddy (rice) | Pulses (greengram), mustard, maize | Continuous paddy 5+ years → Si depletion, fungal load | Diversify to break disease cycle |
| Maize | Pulses, leafy vegetables | Maize-maize (FAW carryover) | FAW pupae survive in soil |
| Cotton | Pulses, sorghum | Cotton-cotton (5+ years) → wilt + soil fatigue | Long-fallow rotation extends Bt life |
| Mango (perennial) | N/A — long rotation | Avoid replanting mango in same pit | Soil pathogen accumulation |
| Eucalyptus (after harvest, after 3 rotations) | Pulses, millets for 2-3 years before any new perennial | Avoid immediate eucalyptus replant | Allelopathy legacy |
| Oil palm (after 25-year cycle) | Replant oil palm only after sanitation | Don't follow with coconut | Shared Ganoderma host |

## Yield Optimizer — Risk-Adjusted Recommendation Framework

The Optimizer should NOT recommend the highest-yield option blindly. The framework should evaluate:

1. **Yield uplift potential** (from baseline).
2. **Investment required** (one-time + recurring).
3. **Risk of failure** (technical complexity, weather dependency).
4. **Payback period** (cash flow concern for smallholders).
5. **Reversibility** (can the farmer go back if it doesn't work?).

### I. Risk-Adjusted Recommendation Tiers

**Tier 1 — Low-risk, high-ROI (recommend for ALL farmers in scope):**
- Paddy: 4-split N schedule + LCC use (₹2,000 incremental, 15-25% yield uplift).
- Paddy: Pheromone trap monitoring (₹2,000, prevents BPH catastrophe).
- Mango: Annual maintenance pruning (₹3,000-5,000/acre, prevents alternate bearing acceleration).
- Maize: FAW pheromone monitoring (₹1,500/acre, prevents 30-50% loss).
- Cotton: Refuge compliance (mandatory; cost-neutral with RIB seed packets).
- All crops: Soil testing every 2-3 years (₹500-1,000, saves ₹3,000-8,000 fertilizer cost).

**Tier 2 — Medium-risk, medium-ROI (recommend with caveats):**
- Paddy: Switch to AWD (water saving + slight yield gain; requires drainage tube management).
- Mango: PBZ in off-year (irreversible if overdosed; requires careful protocol).
- Maize: Intercrop with cowpea (reduces FAW but reduces sole maize yield slightly).
- Cotton: HDPS conversion (high investment but high return; requires drip + management).
- Eucalyptus: First-time planting with ITC contract (5-year commitment; water audit needed).

**Tier 3 — High-risk, high-ROI (only with strong technical support):**
- Paddy: Full SRI (labor-intensive; transplanter needed for scale).
- Mango: Off-season strategy (combines multiple complex interventions).
- Cotton: Ultra-HDPS 45 × 10 cm spacing (extreme density management).
- Eucalyptus: Long rotation (12-15 years pole/timber) with carbon credit certification.
- Oil palm: 25-year commitment (highest commitment; mill distance critical).

### J. The "First-Time Farmer" Adjustment

When a farmer is attempting a crop / technique for the first time, the Yield Optimizer should:

1. **Lower confidence by 20-30%** on yield projections.
2. **Recommend Tier 1 interventions only** in Year 1.
3. **Increase recommended buffer** in cost estimates by 20% (mistakes happen).
4. **Strongly recommend KVK / extension visit** before committing > ₹2 L investment.
5. **Suggest pilot scale** (1-2 acres) before scaling.
